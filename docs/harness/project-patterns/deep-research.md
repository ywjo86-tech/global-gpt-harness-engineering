# Deep Research Harness Pattern

## Purpose

Use this pattern to answer complex research questions with evidence from web sources, academic material, community signals, cross-validation, and a synthesized report.

## When to Use

- The answer may affect a business, technical, product, or investment decision.
- Current information matters.
- Multiple source types are needed.
- Conflicting claims need reconciliation.

## Agent Team Composition

- Research lead: scopes the question and evidence standard.
- Web researcher: gathers current web and official sources.
- Academic researcher: reviews papers, standards, and formal references.
- Community signal analyst: captures practitioner reactions and adoption signals.
- Verification reviewer: cross-checks important claims.
- Synthesis writer: writes the final report.

## Workflow

1. Define the research question, decision context, and output format.
2. Build a source plan covering official, academic, market, and community sources.
3. Search web and official sources for current facts.
4. Search academic or standards sources for durable technical evidence.
5. Review community reactions separately as sentiment or adoption signals.
6. Cross-check claims across independent sources.
7. Label uncertainty, conflicts, and evidence quality.
8. Produce a final synthesis with recommendations and gaps.

## Inputs

- Research question
- Decision context
- Timeframe
- Geography or market scope
- Source constraints
- Required output format

## Outputs

- Research plan
- Source map
- Evidence table or summary
- Cross-check notes
- Synthesized report
- Open questions

## Validation Criteria

- Important claims have citations or source notes.
- Academic and official sources are separated from commentary.
- Community reaction is labeled as signal, not verified fact.
- Conflicting evidence is explained.
- Recommendations include confidence level and uncertainty.

## Cautions

- Do not overstate weak or single-source claims.
- Use current source verification for fast-moving topics.
- Avoid treating popularity as correctness.
- Keep copyrighted source quoting short and use summaries.
